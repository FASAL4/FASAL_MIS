Case Study 1  Collective efforts leading to the dismissal of corrupt   PDS distributors in Fakirpuri  Village  Fa k irpuri   is   a   Tharu - dominated   panchayat  located in Mihipurwa block, a remote T a rai  region   on   the   Indo - Nepal   border   in   the  Bahraich district of eastern Uttar Pradesh.  Despite   the   government's   efforts,   the  panchayat   remained   underdeveloped,   and  various schemes have failed due to corruption.  The panchayat lacked knowledge about   G ram  S abha,   and   the   administrative   corruption  prevented the implementation of the Forest Rights Act 2006, causing it to be caught in the  midst of various problems. These issues included a lack of pure dri nking water, migration,  excessive   use   of   chemicals   in   agriculture,   unemployment,  illiteracy, non - functioning of the public distribution system, drug  abuse, lack of   organisation , depletion of   the   fertility of the land,  the   terror   of   wild   animals,   and   frequent   accidents.  One of th e most significant   and priority   issues faced by Fakirpuri  was the fraudulent practices of the   Kotedar , who was responsible  for the distribution of ration in the panchayat.   Kotedar   Ajay, a  resident of Fakirpuri, was involved in irre gularities and loot of  ration distribution, such as giving only 3 kg of ration instead of the  allotted 5 kg per unit, reducing ration or giving it only once instead  of twice per month, and not distributing mustard oil, salt, chana,  and other rations accord ing to   the   government rules. The agitating  women from the panchayat brought this issue to light during the  monthly meeting of Divya Jyoti Cluster and Mahapanchayat  organised   by the   members   of Divya Jyoti Cluster on 01 - 09 - 2022.  During the meeting, all the aggrieved   G ram   S abha members submitted an application against  the   Kot e dar   through local women leader Bisri Behen, Anarkali, Tarawati, Gulia Devi, Suman,  Gayatri, and Hema, demanding the dismissal of the   Kot e dar   from the village head and the  restoration of a new quota. The members of the gram  panchayat and the village head unanimously found the  application   to   be   true   and   decided   to   dismiss   the  Kot e dar .However , corrupt officials of the administration  were engaged in the loot of the   Kot e dar   for the last five  years, making it a major source of their income. As a  result, even after   multiple   demonstrations by women, no  action was taken, and   Kot e dar   tried his best to suppress  the case by giving lakhs of rupees to corrupt officials. The  government officials also appeared to be in favour of  Kotadar . The women of the village did not stop moving

forward and reached Tehsil Day   at   Mihipurwa for the implementation of the decision.   Since  the illiterate women of the village could not read, they asked some educated   members   among  them to read the text being written by the officer sent by the administration for a false  investigation. Upon reading the report, it was found that the officer had written a report  favouring   the   Kot e dar   and took the mark signature from all the women. All the women drove  away the concerned officer from the village and then reached   the district collector's office,  raising the issue of violation of the Constitution by the district administration. The Panchayat  Raj Act gives the village head the right to investigate against the   Kot e dar , and when the village  head has written the inquiry report, the decision of the   Gr am   P anchayat should not be ignored.  Due to the pressure from the women, the   administration was forced to call a district - level  meeting and dismiss the corrupt   Kotedar . This success story proves that change will come from  the village itself, and only with its leadership will society be able to free itself from the web of  problems. The women of Fakirpuri panchayat proved that change could come from the village  itself. With their strong leadership and determination, they were able  to free their society from the web of problems. The success story   of  Fakirpuri   panchayat   is   an   inspiration   for   other   marginalised  communities struggling with similar challenges.  The key to their success was their collective action and effective use  of the democratic process. The women of the panchayat came  together, raised their voices against the corruption and irregularities  committed by the   Kotedar , and demanded justice. They   organised  meetings,   submitted   petitions,   and   mobilised   other   community  members to join their cause. They  also   held   the   government  accountable   for   their  responsibilities and did not back  down even when officials tried to  suppress their voices.  Another important aspect of their  success   was   their   ability   to  overcome their own barriers, such  as illiteracy, lack of resources, and  social constraints. They worked together to find solutions to their problems and empowered  each other through education and awareness programs.  The successful removal of the corrupt   Koteda r   was a turning point for Fakirpuri   panchayat. It  created a ripple effect of positive change . Other   issues ,   such as lack of pure drinking water,  unemployment, and illiteracy ,   convergence with   the BOCW department are also in the pipeline  to get addressed   with the collective effort of the community. The women of Fakirpuri  panchayat   have   set   an   example   of  effective community participation and  democratic engagement.  Their   success   story   highlights   the  importance   of   empowering  marginalised   communities, particularly  women, and ensuring their participation

in decision - making processes. It also underscores the need for transparency, accountability,  and a strong democratic system to address issues of corruption and inequality. The success of  Fakirpuri   P anchayat is a testament to the power of collective action and community - led change. I won the post of Prime Minister by 13 votes, and since then, I have  been working to ensure the four rights of children: the right to live, the right to development,  the right to security, and the right to partic ipation.  There are 45 families living in our village, and we all come from Scheduled Tribes. We are  still very afraid of the police, especially those in the Tharu caste. Community participation remains crucial for the success of   FASAL,   aimed at improving the  lives of   small and marginal farmers , especially women. However, several factors are currently  hindering the participation of community members in   programme activities , and this is  adversely affecting   the success of   our   programme activities .   Following are the c hallenges  faced   by DEHAT during the   first y ear of FASAL   i mplementation :  •   Alcoholism continues to affect community participation. The use of alcohol is leading  to a reduction in motivation and engagement in community activities. Furthermore,  excessive alcohol use is leading to social and health problems such as domestic violence  and health issues that are further limiting participation in   the programme activities .  •   Resistance by men is still a significant factor that is impacting the participation of  women in   the programme activities .   M en continue to hold significant power and  influence   over   decision - making,   including   decisions   related   to   community  participation. If men resist the involvement of women in interventions, it   limits   their  ability to participate, reducing the impact of   the programme activities . This resistance  may be driven by deep - rooted patriarchal beliefs that men should be the decision -  makers in the co mmunity.

•   Corruption in local governance and administration is also hindering community  participation in interventions. When officials responsible for implementing   legal  provisions/schemes   are corrupt, it   leads   to mismanagement of resources and reduced  trust in the   programme .  •   Domestic violence remains a significant barrier that is limiting women's participation  in interventions. Women who are experiencing violence at home are finding it  challenging to participate in community activities, including   but not limited to AASs  monthly meetings . This is leading to their isolation and limiting their ability to access  services and support.  •   The pressure of household chores is also a significant factor that is limiting women's  participation in interventions.   W omen continue to be responsible for a range of  domestic tasks, including caring for children and elderly family members. This   limits  their ability to participate in community activities, including interventions, reducing  their engagement and limiting the impact of the intervention.  •   Lack of intersectoral coordination among government stakeholders is also a significant  challenge that is impacting community participation   in demanding schemes related to  agriculture and horticulture . When different government agencies responsible for  implementing interventions do not coordinate effectively, it   leads   to confusion and  duplication of effort.  •   Finally, the migration of youth men and their partners who are part of AASs is another  factor that is limiting community participation. When young men and their partners  who are involved in AASs leave the community, it   leads   to reduced engagement and  limited continuity   in the programme activities.
