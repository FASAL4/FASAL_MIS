Section 1: How did it go?

**Narrative on key highlights:**

**Opportunities:**

1\. Strengthening Community-Based Organizations (CBOs):  
a. Opportunity: The formation and empowerment of organizations like the "Sangharsh Block Level Federation" and "Aajeevika Adhikar Sanghathans" at various levels (Hamlet and Gram Panchayat) have significantly enhanced the community's capacity to engage in self-governance and economic self-determination.

b. Achievement: These CBOs have established a strong identity within the community by demonstrating tangible results, earning trust, and gaining recognition from government officials, further legitimized by their voluntary contributions, such as providing office space.

2\. Empowering Women in Local Governance:  
a. Opportunity: The program has effectively leveraged advocacy initiatives to significantly increase the participation of women in the governance processes at various administrative levels.

b. Achievement: Women's consistent and high participation rates in meetings (exceeding 50%) have been pivotal in enhancing their influence and role in decision-making processes, crucial for advancing gender equality and inclusive governance. The advocacy efforts have led to a collective leverage amount of more than ₹2.5 Crore from government resources in the reported HYs, bringing the total number to more than ₹ 3.04 Crore, including the previous HY's numbers.

3\. Economic Stability through LIESA-based Natural Agriculture:  
a. Opportunity: Implementing Low External Input Sustainable Agriculture (LIESA) has proven effective in increasing the economic stability of participating families.

b. Achievement: The increased income from LIESA-based practices has motivated more families to join and engage with CBOs, thereby spreading the adoption of sustainable and economically beneficial agricultural practices across the community.

4\. Addressing Community Expectations and Ownership:  
a. Opportunity: The evolving role of CBOs presents an opportunity to further educate and unify the community around the principles of collective action and shared benefits.

b. Achievement: The program's focus on maintaining a community-centred ethos has been essential in managing expectations about personal gains versus collective benefits, thereby fostering a more cohesive community approach.

**Challenges:**

1\. Intrusion of Exploitative Organisations:  
o What: Entry of organisations that exploit local resources and communities and corrupt the culture of volunteerism and sense of community among the citizens of DEHAT's intervention villages. E.g., The organisations are selling sanitary pads, which is to be leveraged from Nyaya Panchayat Resource Center through advocacy efforts, maternal and child wellness products, which is to be leveraged from Anganwaadi Centers through advocacy efforts, and fake organic pesticides and fertilisers, which can be made at home for free with the given natural resources. The organisations sell through AAS members and CRPs by appointing them as sales agents on commission within their communities. Our approach is not selling but facilitating the advocacy efforts to leverage it from available government resources or make agriculture-based commodities possible at home.

o Why: These organisations undermine rights-based ethics and efforts and exploit community members, which erodes trust in any community-led developmental work.

o Mitigation: Strengthening local governance structures and empowering CBOs to better regulate and control external influences through legal, economic, ethical and moral awareness and advocacy.

2\. Land Ownership and Use Issues:  
o What: Some families have farming land in flood-prone river areas, while some are landless or take a small piece on lease/rent for their livelihood survival.

o Why: These conditions severely restrict economic opportunities which can be leveraged from government schemes and LIESA-based Natural Agriculture.

o Mitigation: Integrating FPOs to support landless farm labourers and supporting them for LIESA-based Natural Agriculture to be applied on rooftops and kitchen gardens.  
  
3\. Challenges in Women's Participation in Labor:  
o What: Women are unable to attend important meetings due to their household-level chores or other labour commitments.

o Why: This limits their participation in decision-making processes and reduces their influence on community development.

o Mitigation: Scheduling meetings at times that are more convenient for women and incentivising their attendance through registering them for eligible rights and entitlements to encourage their participation.

4\. Corruption and Misuse of Power:  
o What: Extreme violation of Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989, JJ Act, 2015, The Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006 and Bonded Labour System (Abolition)Act, 1976 by the local police and forest department exerting undue influence and falsely implicating community members.

o Why: Corruption and power misuse lead to mistrust in authorities and hinder effective governance.

o Mitigation: Building resilience through continuous one-on-one meetings and facilitating advocacy initiatives at the Taluka and District level through formal complaints to the Forest Department and Police Department. The Block Level Federation is preparing to file a judicial complaint in the relevant court based on the ground evidence inspired by the recent Tamil Nadu case (https://www.thehindu.com/opinion/lead/the-triumph-of-vachathi-over-a-hostile-state/article67377001.ece) in the near future.

5\. Corrupt Practices in Government Schemes:  
o What: Government employees only provide benefits to a small fraction of eligible people without bribes.

o Why: Such practices diminish trust in public services and discourage community engagement with government programs.

o Mitigation: Implement monitoring groups comprised of community members to oversee the distribution of government services and facilitate CBOs to advocate for fair practices.

6\. Chemical Intensive non-technical vs. LIESA-based Natural Farming Practices:  
o What: Reluctance to adopt organic farming practices due to a perception that traditional chemical methods yield more.

o Why: This reluctance can hinder the transition to sustainable agricultural practices that are better for the environment and long-term productivity.

o Mitigation: Capacity Building on the benefits of organic farming, demonstrating its long-term advantages for soil health and productivity, and facilitating initial subsidies or incentives through the Horticulture Department and APPI-supported input support to reduce the perceived risk.

7\. Media Hesitancy and Coverage  
o What: The program encounters challenges with media hesitancy, where local journalists are reluctant to report on aforementioned issues or the program's initiatives due to fear of repercussions or external pressures.  
o Why: This issue is critical because effective and honest media coverage is essential for transparency, public awareness and facilitating pressure groups pertaining to rights-based issues. Hesitant media leads to underreported achievements and challenges the community faces, leading to misinformation and a lack of necessary administrative support and understanding.

Mitigation:  
o Building Trust with Journalists: Foster a safe and trusting environment for journalists by engaging them in open dialogues, offering clear and factual information, and ensuring they understand the community's and program's objectives. Regular briefings and press meets are set up to keep the media informed and involved.

o Empowering Community Voices: Train community members and CBO leaders in media literacy and communication skills to directly engage with the broader public through social media and other platforms. This reduces reliance on traditional media and promotes grassroots information dissemination.

o Collaborations with Credible Media Outlets: Collaboration with regional or national media outlets with a track record of unbiased and fearless reporting. These collaborations help amplify the correct narratives and bring regional issues to a broader audience, compensating for local media hesitancy.

**Have you forged any new partnerships, funders or linkages during the last reporting period of the programme?**

In addition to the partnerships mentioned in the previous year, we have moved forward with the following for the upcoming years:

1\. Department of Agriculture and Horticulture, Bahraich, Govt. of Uttar Pradesh:

Partnership Details: The program partnered with the Department of Agriculture and Horticulture, which has provided subsidised input support and enhanced agricultural practices within the community.

Impact: This partnership has provided the community with access to expert advice and resources, helping to promote more sustainable and productive agricultural practices.

2\. Incubation & Innovation Centre, IIT Kanpur:

Partnership Details: Collaboration with IIT Kanpur was initiated to support the farmers with certified organic fertilisers with content/kg; Carbon 74%, Nitrogen 276 kg/ha, Phosphorus, 12 kg/ha, Potassium 162 kg/ha, Sulphur 11 ppm, Zinc 0.87 ppm, Iron 4.0 ppm, Copper, 2.7 ppm, Manganese, 3.8 ppm which has the potential to match the productivity rate of chemical fertilisers in addition to the following but not limited to:

a. Soil Structure  
Because of the organic matter in organic fertiliser, soil structure is improved, resulting in the soil's ability to hold onto water and nutrients increases.

b. Reduce Fertilisers and Pesticide  
Although organic fertiliser can be more costly than synthetic, it can reduce the need for pesticides and the overall nitrogen, Phosphorus, and potassium requirements. Because of the reductions, organic fertiliser can be cost neutral and sometimes a cost savings.

c. Plant Damage Threat Avoided  
Some synthetic fertilisers can cause plant damage to leaves and roots. This is less likely with organic fertilisers.

Impact: This linkage has facilitated access to organic fertiliser for the intervention farmers.

3\. Janvikas CBO Incubation Program, Ahmedabad, Gujrat (2024 HY2 onwards):

Partnership Details: Selected members of the "Sangharsh Block Level Federation" will be capacitated and mentored to build the rights-based movement, including the aspects of socio-legal, socio-political, and socio-economical resistance and resilience in the coming days.

Impact: The partnership will equip the BLF members with essential skills to address the aforementioned issues in a self-sustainable manner beyond the duration of the project support.

4\. Sambhavna Institute of Public Policy and Politics (2024 HY2 onwards):

Partnership Details:  
Partners: Jaya and Zubair from the Sambhavna Institute of Public Policy and Politics.  
Program: Theatre of the Oppressed, integrating interactive theatre techniques to address socio-legal and socio-legal issues.

Impact:  
a. Community Engagement: The program empowers community members, particularly women and marginalised groups, to express concerns and advocate for change through role-playing and scenario-based activities.  
b. Educational and Empowerment Tool: This creative approach facilitates education and dialogue, allowing participants to visualise solutions to their challenges.  
c. Capacity Building: Enhances the program's ability to engage community members in discussions about public policy and politics.

Implementation Strategy:  
a. Workshops and Sessions: The partners will facilitate quarterly workshops, integrating theatre activities with the programme's ongoing rights and entitlements activities.  
b. Monitoring and Evaluation: Assess the effectiveness in fostering dialogue and promoting civic participation.
