Introduction  Uttar Pradesh is one of the most underdeveloped states in India, particularly in its eastern  region, which faces severe challenges due to high population density, small landholdings,  limited industrialization, and frequent flooding. Bahraich district, loca ted in this region, ranks  among the poorest in the state, positioned 69th in terms of poverty. The district's Human  Development Index (HDI) is low at 0.446. Land ownership patterns in Bahraich are skewed,  with 13% of households classified as smallholders,   67% as marginal, and 12% as landless.  The area’s economic struggles have led NITI Aayog to designate Bahraich as one of India’s  117 aspirational districts that require urgent government intervention.  Mihinpurwa, a remote block within Bahraich district, is situated near the Indo - Nepal border  and faces unique geographical and socio - economic challenges. Surrounded by dense forests  and bordered by the Saryu and Sharda rivers, the area is prone to frequent   flooding despite  its fertile land. The block's border location has fostered cross - border interactions, influencing  its economic and social fabric.  Agriculture is the primary economic activity in Mihinpurwa, with most farmers relying on  traditional cultivation methods, primarily growing wheat, rice, and a few vegetables, along  with some livestock rearing. However, the area suffers from low productivit y, limited access  to modern farming techniques, and poor market linkages, resulting in low agricultural  income. The lack of organized marketing facilities forces farmers to sell their produce at  below - market prices, worsening their financial struggles.  Mihinpurwa also faces numerous social issues, including high poverty, illiteracy, and  inadequate access to essential services such as healthcare and education. The proximity to  the Indo - Nepal border increases the community's vulnerability to child traffick ing, human  trafficking, and child labor. Many residents migrate to nearby cities or other states in search  of better opportunities, often encountering exploitative conditions. Gender - based  discrimination is common, limiting women’s involvement in economic   activities and their  access to healthcare and education.  DEHAT, a well - established NGO operating in Mihinpurwa for several years, has launched a  new initiative in response to the community's needs, supported by the Azim Premji  Foundation. This program aims to boost agricultural income and ensure nutritional secu rity  for the community in Mihinpurwa. It also focuses on creating sustainable community - led  institutions and improving access to rights, entitlements, and government schemes.  Purpose of the Study  The purpose of this baseline survey, conducted by DEHAT under the Monitoring &  Evaluation (M&E) framework, is to collect baseline data from target farmers. The survey  results will establish reference data for selected indicators relevant to the farmers  par ticipating in the project. This baseline data will serve as a benchmark for comparing the  project's impact, particularly in terms of increasing farmers' income through various project  interventions, when an end - line survey is conducted in the future.

Rationale for the   Study  The agriculture baseline survey aims to assess the current agricultural landscape of the  intervention blocks and villages targeted by the Fasal project. The findings will help to  create a model that highlights existing agricultural practices in the project   area. Based on  these insights, an effective agriculture strategy will be developed to encourage farmers to  adopt suitable and sustainable agricultural practices. This will not only enhance their land  productivity but also promote diversification into prec ision farming. The survey will establish  baseline benchmarks for key indicators, which will be periodically reviewed to measure  progress.  Scope of the Study  The study is geographically limited to the Mihinpurwa block, where the project is being  implemented. It will cover the current status of agriculture, livestock, and access to  government schemes among small and marginal farmers in the block. Analysis of Crop Economics: Cost, Production, and Income  Kharif Season  In the Kharif (Rainy) season, paddy is the predominant crop, grown by 71.2% of farmers  and covering 73% of the total cultivable land. Despite its importance, paddy offers limited  profitability, with a per - acre   net   income of ₹2,720.30 against a production cost of  ₹10,449.36, significantly lower than the state average in Uttar Pradesh (UP), where paddy  yields and incomes are typically higher.   Paddy typically has a yield of about 20 quintals per  acre in UP, with costs generally ranging from ₹15,000 to ₹20,000 per acre and an average  income around ₹30,000 per acre .   This discrepancy suggests challenges such as lower  productivity or market price issues that affect local farmers.  Sugarcane, on the other hand, is highly profitable and aligns well with state trends. It is  cultivated by   only   12.7% of farmers and generates substantial income due to high yields  and market demand, making it a valuable cash crop.   In UP, sugarcane is highly profitable,  with average yields of 450 - 500 quintals per acre, costs around ₹50,000 to ₹60,000, and  incomes upwards of ₹80,000 per acre, aligning closely with   production and rate of project  area.  Sorghum (Jawar) and groundnut provide additional diversity, though their profitability  varies. Groundnut, in particular, shows good returns, highlighting its potential as a  supplementary income source. Compared to Bahraich district averages, the Kharif cro p  choices reflect a   mixture   of tradition and market responsiveness, yet issues like high input  costs and variable market prices impact overall profitability. Enhancing access to improved  seeds, water management, and market linkages could help optimize returns .

Analysis of Chemical Fertilizer Use in Crop Cultivation  The use of chemical fertilizers is   dominant   among farmers in the area, particularly for the  major crops, paddy and wheat. This heavy reliance on chemical inputs reflects the farmers'  attempts to maximize yields, but it also raises concerns about sustainability, soil health, and  long - term environmen tal impacts. The data shows that a significant percentage of farmers  use urea, with 70.62% applying it in paddy cultivation and 75.83% in wheat. This  widespread use of urea highlights its role as a primary nitrogen source, though it often leads  to imbalanc ed nutrient management and potential soil degradation over time.  DAP   is also commonly used, with 67.77% of farmers applying it in paddy fields and 73.46%  in wheat fields. This indicates a strong reliance on phosphate - based fertilizers to support  crop growth, especially during the critical phases of crop development. MOP   and SSP  (Single Super Phosphate) are used to a lesser extent but still play a significant role, with  MOP applied by 44.08% of paddy farmers and 40.76% of wheat farmers, and SSP used by  53.55% and 45.02% of farmers, respectively. These inputs are critical   for potassium and  phosphorus supplementation but contribute to the overall chemical load in the soil.  Interestingly, the use of organic alternatives like FYM (Farm Yard Manure) and bio - fertilizers  remains minimal, with only 10.43% of paddy farmers and 11.85% of wheat farmers using  FYM, and a mere 1.90% and 0.95% using bio - fertilizers, respectively. This lo w adoption rate  of organic inputs suggests a gap in knowledge, availability, or perceived effectiveness  among farmers. Green manuring practices are nearly nonexistent, with only 2.84% of paddy  farmers and 0.47% of wheat farmers employing this sustainable p ractice, indicating a  missed opportunity to improve soil fertility naturally.  The prevalent use of chemical nutrients through foliar sprays, utilized by 38.39% of farmers  in both paddy and wheat, further underscores the dependency on chemical inputs to boost  crop health and productivity. However, the overall low adoption of sustaina ble practices like  green manuring and organic fertilization points to the need for increased awareness and  training on the benefits of integrated nutrient management. Promoting balanced fertilizer  use, encouraging organic amendments, and demonstrating the   long - term advantages of  sustainable practices could help shift the current trends towards more environmentally  friendly and soil - conscious farming methods. Adopt Integrated Nutrient Management (INM):  Integrated Nutrient Management should be promoted to balance the use of chemical and  organic fertilizers, enhancing soil health and reducing environmental impacts. Programs that  educate farmers on the benefits of using Farm Yard Manure (FYM), compost, and   bio -  fertilizers, combined with appropriate doses of chemical fertilizers, can lead to more  sustainable farming. Establishing local composting units and supplying bio - fertilizers at  subsidized rates can encourage widespread adoption.  4.   Enhance Soil Health Through Green Manuring and Organic Practices:  Introducing green manuring practices and increasing the use of organic inputs can  significantly improve soil fertility and reduce the dependency on synthetic fertilizers.  Demonstrations, training, and the provision of green manure seeds can help farmers  un derstand and adopt these sustainable practices. Promoting organic farming through  certification and linking farmers to premium markets can also provide economic incentives.  5.   Encourage Participation in Community Institutions:  Strengthening participation in Self - Help Groups (SHGs) AAS/ producer groups   and other  community institutions can enhance collective bargaining, resource sharing, and access to  credit and inputs. Encouraging active engagement in these groups through awareness  programs and capacity - building initiatives can improve the overall resil ience of the farming  community.

6.   Enhance Market Linkages and Value Chain Development:  Developing better market linkages and supporting value chain initiatives can help farmers  achieve better prices for their produce. Facilitating access to local and regional markets,  promoting direct farmer - to - consumer sales, and supporting the establishmen t of farmer  producer organizations (FPOs) can enhance income opportunities. Value addition through  processing and packaging can further increase profitability.  7.   Implement Policy Support and Incentives for Sustainable Farming:  Advocacy for policy measures that support sustainable farming practices, including subsidies  for organic inputs, incentives for water - saving technologies, and financial rewards for low -  input agriculture, can drive the adoption of environmentally friendly f arming methods.  Policies that promote sustainable agriculture as a priority will encourage farmers to  transition towards practices that benefit both their livelihoods and the environment.
