2.  ANNUAL PROGRAMME UPDATE
What  are  the  key  activities  undertaken  during  the  review  period?  Please  share  your
assessment of how the program is progressing towards planned goals/effects. (For Y1, it

During the reporting period of January to December 2025, the FASAL programme continued
implementation across its core thematic pillars of community institution building, LEISA-based
sustainable  agriculture  and  natural  resource  management,  gender  and  leadership
development, and access to rights and entitlements. The year reflects a consolidation phase

where earlier investments in community organisation and capacity building began translating into
more structured governance engagement, technical strengthening of agriculture practices, and
visible material improvements in villages.
The programme continued to work in 06 Gram Panchayats and 20 villages, covering
approximately 1500 marginal farmer families, as consistently documented in the monthly review
meetings and programme monitoring records.
1. Community Organisation and Institutional Strengthening
A central focus during 2025 was the continued strengthening of the three-tier institutional structure
comprising Aajeevika Adhikar Sangathans (AAS) at the village level, cluster-level collectives,
and the Block Level Federation (BLF).
At the beginning of the year, the programme documented 80 AAS, 05 clusters, and 01 Block Level
Federation across the programme villages. During the course of implementation, the institutional
structure was consolidated to 74 AAS while maintaining 05 clusters and 01 BLF, reflecting a
process of rationalisation aimed at strengthening participation and functionality within the
institutions.
Regular AAS meetings, cluster discussions, Community Resource Person (CRP) monthly
meetings, and Block Level Federation meetings were facilitated throughout the year. These
institutional forums enabled community members to collectively reflect on issues related to agro-
based livelihoods, rights and entitlements, and village-level infrastructure concerns.
Monthly review documentation shows that these platforms were increasingly used for:
• Identifying village-level issues,
• Planning seasonal agricultural activities,
• Tracking applications for government schemes,
• Preparing written submissions such as MNREGA work demand letters, and
• Escalating issues through village, block, and district-level interface processes.
Examples recorded in meeting agendas include collective discussions on road conditions,
drainage issues (nala concerns), and demand generation for employment under MNREGA.
Over the course of the year, these forums gradually shifted from primarily information-sharing
spaces to platforms for collective decision-making and follow-up actions.
Assessment of progress:
This progression reflects strengthening community-led institutions capable of engaging
governance systems. The shift from nominal membership structures to more functional
participation suggests that institutional capacities are maturing and increasingly supporting
programme outcomes related to entitlement access, collective problem solving, and agricultural
planning.

2. Gender Trainings and Leadership Development
Gender and leadership development remained an important component of programme activities
during 2025. Leadership trainings and gender-focused discussions were organised through
institutional platforms and documented regularly in the monthly review materials. These sessions
addressed themes such as women’s leadership, participation in community institutions,
access to rights and entitlements, and engagement with local governance systems.
An important transition observed during this phase is that earlier in the programme cycle,
organisational leadership and programme staff played a more intensive role in facilitating
leadership capacity-building sessions. During 2025, however, women leaders from earlier
training cohorts increasingly took on facilitation roles themselves. Drawing from their own
experiences of participation in Sangathan processes, engagement with government departments,
and follow-up on schemes, these women facilitated discussions with other members within
institutional spaces.
This peer-led process strengthened confidence among newer women leaders and reinforced the
belief that leadership can emerge from within the community. The monthly review documentation
also records increasing participation of women in AAS meetings, interface discussions, and
village-level planning processes.
The reporting period culminated in district-level interface engagement, alongside media
visibility around gender training and leadership development activities.
Assessment of progress:
Baseline assessments had highlighted the limited participation of community members,
particularly women, in collective decision-making spaces despite the presence of membership
structures. The developments observed during 2025 indicate a gradual movement toward women
farmers emerging as active leaders and advocates within community institutions. The shift from
trainer-led sessions to peer-led facilitation represents an important step toward embedding
leadership capacities within the institutions themselves.
3. Promotion of Sustainable Agriculture and Natural Resource Management
During 2025, the programme continued its emphasis on Low External Input Ecological and
Sustainable Agriculture (LEISA) and Natural Resource Management. The programme’s change
pathway of supporting farmers in transitioning from High External Input Sustainable Agriculture
(HEISA) toward Low External Input Sustainable Agriculture is evident in the farming culture of
the community.
Trainings, demonstrations, and follow-up technical sessions were conducted to strengthen
farmers’ knowledge of locally suitable agricultural practices. A major example documented during
the reporting period is the structured training cycle on turmeric cultivation, which was
conducted across multiple villages.

crop choices and external inputs. During 2025, the programme strengthened the agricultural
transition pathway through repeated technical sessions and measurable convergence with the
horticulture department. These developments contribute to building more diversified and resilient
agro-based livelihood systems.

Means of Verification and Monitoring of Activities
The programme follows a structured monitoring system to track activities, outputs, and emerging
outcomes across the implementation period. Monitoring and verification are carried out through a
combination of community-level documentation, programme review systems, and evidence-
based records maintained by the programme team.
1. Institutional Records and Meeting Registers
At the village level, Aajeevika Adhikar Sangathan (AAS) meeting registers are maintained to
record discussions, decisions, and follow-up actions related to agriculture planning, scheme
access, and village infrastructure issues. Similar records are maintained for cluster meetings,
Community Resource Person (CRP) monthly meetings, and Block Level Federation meetings.
These registers serve as primary documentation of:
• Participation of community members in institutional meetings,
• Issues identified and prioritised by communities,
• Collective decisions taken during meetings, and
• Follow-up actions such as scheme applications and written submissions.
Block-level registers also maintain supporting documentation related to programme engagement
with governance systems, including newspaper clippings, official receipts or
acknowledgements from government officials, and copies of applications submitted during
interface meetings at block and district levels.
2. Monthly Review Meetings
The programme conducts Monthly Review Meetings (MRM) with the field team to review
progress across programme activities. These meetings consolidate field updates from village-
level institutions and track developments related to:
• Agricultural training sessions and seasonal crop planning,
• Leadership and gender training activities,
• Village-level interface meetings and governance engagement, and
• Scheme convergence and infrastructure follow-up.
The monthly review documentation serves as an important internal monitoring mechanism for
tracking programme progress and identifying emerging issues requiring follow-up.
3. Training and Capacity Building Documentation
Leadership trainings, gender sessions, and agricultural capacity-building activities are verified
through structured documentation maintained by the programme team. These include:
Page 14 of 39

• Participant attendance lists,
• Training agendas and session reports, and
• Photographic documentation of training activities.
These records help verify the implementation of training programmes and participation of
community members in leadership and agricultural capacity-building initiatives.
4. Photographic and Field Documentation
Field activities and outcomes are supported by photographic documentation, particularly for
activities such as:
• Agricultural training sessions and demonstrations,
• Leadership and gender training programmes,
• Village and district-level interface meetings, and
• Infrastructure improvements such as roads, drainage systems, and water supply facilities.
These photographs provide visual evidence of programme implementation and outcomes and are
maintained as part of the programme documentation.
5. Agriculture Intervention Records
Agricultural interventions promoted under the programme are verified through field-level
documentation maintained during implementation. These include:
• Input distribution lists of participating farmers,
• Photographs of input distribution activities, and
• Photo or video documentation demonstrating adoption of promoted agricultural practices
and packages of practices.
These records help verify the participation of farmers in agricultural interventions and track field-
level implementation of programme-supported practices.
6. Scheme Tracking and Leverage Documentation
The programme maintains a leverage tracking system to document access to government
schemes and community infrastructure improvements facilitated through institutional processes.
The leverage documentation records:
• Village-wise scheme access,
• Type of entitlement or infrastructure accessed,
• Number of Right-holders, and
• Financial value of the scheme or infrastructure.
This system allows the programme to monitor the scale of convergence achieved and assess
progress toward improving access to public resources and services.

7. Household-Level Monitoring through the Family Development Booklet
The Family Development Booklet (FDB) serves as a key tool for tracking household-level progress
related to agriculture, livelihoods, and access to rights and entitlements. These booklets are
maintained physically by farmers and capture information related to:
• Cropping patterns and agricultural practices,
• Livelihood diversification activities,
• Scheme access and entitlements, and
• Household-level food and nutrition indicators.
The booklet enables families to maintain their own record of development progress while also
supporting programme-level monitoring.
To complement physical records, the programme is using the Kobo Collect application to digitise
data from the Family Development Booklet. This integration of physical and digital tools enables
more systematic data management, improves the accuracy of monitoring, and allows the
programme team to track progress across households and villages more effectively.
Programme facilitators and Community Resource Persons (CRPs) regularly engage with
community institutions to monitor implementation progress, support follow-up on applications
and entitlements, and provide feedback during review meetings.
This ongoing field engagement ensures that issues identified in village-level meetings are tracked
and addressed through appropriate institutional or administrative channels.
Together, these mechanisms provide a multi-layered monitoring system combining community-
level documentation, programme review processes, and household-level data tracking tools.
Emerging insights from the Family Development Booklet data, triangulated with findings from the
Midline Assessment, indicate encouraging trends in agricultural practices, livelihood
diversification, and household resilience. Monitoring evidence shows increasing crop
diversification, expanded vegetable cultivation, improved cropping intensity through practices
such as Zaid-season cultivation and intercropping, and improved dietary diversity at the household
level.
The Midline Assessment also indicates a gradual shift in household income categories and
increased uptake of government services related to education, social welfare, health, and
employment schemes. These findings suggest stronger engagement between communities and
public systems.
The convergence of institutional documentation, monitoring tools, and assessment findings
provides credible evidence that the programme is progressing toward its intended outcomes of

enhanced livelihood security, improved nutrition, stronger engagement with governance

1.  Organisational, Programmatic and Financial Challenges
The FASAL programme operates in the forest-fringe and border villages of the Bahraich district
in Uttar Pradesh, particularly around the Katarniaghat Wildlife Sanctuary and Indo–Nepal
border  region.  Communities  in  this  geography  experience  multiple  structural  vulnerabilities,
including chronic poverty, human–wildlife conflict, unsafe migration, trafficking risks, child
marriage, and limited access to public services and governance systems.
Working  within  this  context  requires  navigating  a  complex  ecosystem  of  institutional,  socio-
political, and financial challenges. During the reporting period, DEHAT faced challenges related to
organisational leadership transition, human resource retention, financial sustainability, and
resistance within local governance structures. At the same time, the organisation continued to
strengthen institutional leadership, expand governance partnerships, and deepen its engagement
with  national  and  international  networks  working  on  gender  justice,  child  protection,  and
community-led development.

2. Organisational Leadership Transition and Institutional Strengthening
Over the past few years, DEHAT has been undergoing a transition from a founder-led organisation
to a collective and decentralised leadership structure. This transition required significant
institutional restructuring to ensure continuity of programmes while strengthening shared
leadership across the organisation.
To institutionalise this process, DEHAT established a Strategic Leadership Team (SLT) and Core
Team, which collectively guide programme strategy, organisational development, and institutional
governance.
This leadership transition has been supported through an ongoing Organisational Development
process initiated in 2021 with the guidance of the veteran Organisation Development
facilitator, Gagan Sethi. The process focuses on strengthening:
• Decentralised and feminist leadership practices
• Collective decision-making processes
• Internal accountability systems
• Two-way accountability between organisational leadership and community institutions.
Through this structured transition process, the organisation has gradually strengthened its
institutional resilience and governance capacity.
3. Human Resource and Institutional Capacity Challenges
Recruitment and retention of programme staff remain a challenge due to the remote location of
intervention areas, limited infrastructure, and safety concerns in certain forest and border
villages.

partners — Caritas and She’s the First (STF) were unable to continue their previously committed

Case Study: My Life Story
Shanti
I. Childhood and the Loss of a Father
"Papa passed away a long time ago. At that time, we were very small. After Papa left, our whole
world changed. All the responsibilities of the house fell on Mummy. Mummy never expressed
her grief before us. She made herself strong and wanted to make us strong too. Mummy worked
very hard to educate us. There were times when there was no money in the house, but Mummy
never let our studies stop. She worked herself but never let us do manual labor. She never let
our hands leave our books."
II. The Trap of Child Marriage
"Today, whatever I am, it includes Mummy’s penance, sacrifice, and hard work. But I was
married off at a very young age. It was a child marriage. At that time, I didn’t know my age, nor
did I understand my rights. In childhood itself, the burden of responsibilities fell on my
shoulders. While leaving my mother’s house, my heart was full of fear, but no one listened to
me."
Page 29 of 39

III. The Dark Years of Abuse
"After reaching my in-laws' house, my sorrows increased. I didn’t find love or belonging there.
Every day was spent in taunts, insults, and pain. My husband’s behaviour was very harsh. There
was physical abuse over small things. My soul was more wounded than my body.
I had to bear physical and mental torture. Questions were raised about my body and my being
a woman. Because my periods didn't come on time, I was called insulting words like 'Kinnar'
(transgender). These things were said not just by outsiders, but by my own people. It is difficult
to describe this pain in words."
IV. Abandonment and the "Burden"
"Things got so bad that my husband left me and went away. Without any responsibility, no one
was giving me support. I stayed in that condition for two years. Then I came back to my mother’s
house, but I didn't find peace there either. My own family gave me a lot of pain. I was considered
a 'burden.' The eyes of society and the indifference of my own people—everything felt like it was
biting me at once. Many times it felt like I didn't have the strength to bear it anymore."
V. A Spark of Hope: The Gift of Motherhood
"After some time, my husband came back. I thought maybe now conditions would change. In
the meantime, we had two children. Their arrival brought a little light into my life; I saw a reason
to live in their faces. I thought that now everything would be fine. But perhaps destiny didn't
approve of me being settled. The struggle was not taking the name of ending. In the middle of
that struggle, I lost both my children.
This was the biggest and deepest wound of my life. To be a mother and lose your children is the
greatest sorrow. I was completely broken from the inside. But still, in some corner, a small spark
was left. That spark would make me stand up again after falling.
Then another turn came. A woman gave birth to a girl and died. That little girl was left alone in
the world. At that time, I forgot all my sorrows and decided to adopt that girl. This decision came
from the heart, not out of any compulsion. With her arrival, my lonely life found its glow again. I
became a mother once more. I accepted her from my heart without thinking about what society
would say."
VI. The Intervention: Meeting Manju and DEHAT
"Inside the house, the same chaos and violence continued. My husband used to beat and insult
me. In the middle of this, an NGO came to our village. Initially, I also wanted to go there, but I
couldn't because of the fear of the house. But one day I went. When I came back, my husband
beat me a lot.
Page 30 of 39

But there I met a woman from the village whose name was Manju. Manju looked at me carefully
for the first time and listened to me. Without knowing who I was, she understood my pain and
assured me that 'We will move forward like this; you are not alone.' It was with her support and
courage that I started getting involved in the activities of DEHAT."
VII. Learning and Empowerment
"Through Manju, I got the chance to understand the outside world. I realised that my life is not
limited only to fear and beatings. By connecting with Manju and DEHAT, confidence slowly
began to build inside me. Manju supported me at every step. She taught me the importance of
the children's education and helped me study too.
Whenever I used to lose courage, she would handle me. It is with her cooperation that I could
study again. Once, she even scolded me because I used to send voice recordings. She told me,
'We cannot listen to your recordings in front of everyone. Learn to message.' She was the one
who taught me how to message. And whenever I got time, I would read."

"Now I can read and write. It is because of her that we have moved forward. Manju and I are the
first women of the village who have moved forward like this. Both of us have sent our daughters
to the Kasturba School from the village for the first time. Now, many people in the village have
started sending their daughters.
Now I am not afraid of anyone. I can talk to everyone in the best way and will learn even more.
This support has not only given us the strength to move forward in society but has also given us
a new hope regarding the future of our children. Slowly, I have started feeling that my life is now
not just a story of pain, but a story of emerging from struggle and moving forward."

Case Study: A Story of Struggle and Self-Reliance
Manju
I. The Beginning: A Fire Within (2016)
"The village mornings were always full of dust and hopes. In that same village, an ordinary woman

"We have a total of three bighas of land. To hear it, three bighas might seem large, but its reality was

Now we had so much courage that we could take a risk. We decided that we will plant sugarcane
in all three bighas. Sugarcane is such a crop that can bear water and also give a good income. In
the first year, there was fear—what if the water fills up again? But this time, hard work and fate were
both together. Water did come into the khala land, but the sugarcane bore it. On the highland, the
crop was even better."
VI. Results: From Burden to Income
"When we plant sugarcane in the field, we also plant some vegetables in it so the house runs well.
When the green sugarcane swayed, it felt like our hard work was smiling. For two years, we have
been continuously planting only sugarcane in all three bighas. But the seeds that we get
from DEHAT, we plant those in the fields as well. Now that the same land which used to give worry
has started giving hope. The two bighas of khala land are now not a burden, but have become a
medium of income."
VII. Conclusion: The Real Victory
"Small savings of the group gave birth to big dreams. Some women stood on their own feet, and
some started small businesses. And the one who started this journey is now not just a member but
has become an inspiration. The income may be less, but the experience gained is very valuable.
Respect was found. Identity was found. This journey from 2016 until today was not just about
earning money; this journey was about becoming self-reliant. And the story has not ended yet,
because the real victory is the one that is found through struggle."

Case Study: The Inspiring Journey of Anarkali
From Social Discrimination to an Exemplar of Leadership and Organic Farming
1. Introduction and Background
Anarkali is a resident of Rampurwa village, Fakirpuri Post, in Bahraich district of Uttar Pradesh. She
currently serves as the Treasurer of the Sadhana Azadi Aajivika Adhikar Sangathan (AAS). As a
member of a tribal community, her early life was shaped by deep social discrimination and
structural inequalities, as communities like hers were historically placed at the margins of society.
In her community, women were rarely made aware of their rights and were provided very limited
opportunities for education, mobility, or economic independence. As a result, Anarkali lived for
many years in an environment where women’s voices were rarely heard. She often lacked the
confidence to speak publicly or challenge social norms. Yet, despite these challenges, she carried
a strong aspiration to build an identity for herself and contribute positively to her community
2. The Path to Leadership: DEHAT and AAS
Anarkali’s leadership potential was first recognised by DEHAT, which provided her with an
opportunity to engage in community processes and strengthen her capabilities. Through DEHAT,
she became associated with the Aajivika Adhikar Sangathan (AAS), a community platform that
focuses on building awareness around rights, livelihoods, and collective action.
Joining AAS proved to be a turning point in her life. Through collective discussions and community
mobilisation, she realised that social change requires organised efforts and collective struggle.
Gradually, she began raising her voice in community forums and encouraging other women to
become aware of their rights and opportunities for self-reliance. Her engagement contributed to
strengthening collective participation among women in the village.
3. Struggles for Social and Government Rights
Anarkali played a key role in advocating for the community’s access to essential government
welfare schemes, where many families had earlier faced exclusion or discrimination.
Ration Distribution:
She raised concerns about irregularities and discriminatory practices in the Public Distribution
System (PDS). By engaging with local authorities, she helped establish monitoring mechanisms
that ensured eligible families began receiving ration supplies on time.
MGNREGA and Employment:
She actively advocated for improved implementation of the MGNREGA programme. Through
repeated engagement with officials, she demanded quality employment opportunities and timely
payment of wages for workers in her community.

Case Study: Rajkumari Devi
From Wage Labour to a Leading Woman Farmer
1. Introduction and Background
Rajkumari Devi is a resident of Kailash Nagar village in Mihinpurwa block of Bahraich district, Uttar
Pradesh. For many years, she supported her family through daily wage labour as the small plot of
agricultural land owned by the household did not generate sufficient income. Farming was
practiced using traditional methods and largely remained a subsistence activity rather than a
reliable livelihood source.
Like many smallholder farmers in the region, Rajkumari Devi faced multiple constraints, including
limited access to technical knowledge, low crop productivity, and unpredictable agricultural
income. Despite these challenges, she remained determined to improve her family’s economic
situation and explore better ways of using her land.
2. The Challenge: Limited Agricultural Productivity and Livelihood Insecurity
Before engaging with the programme, agriculture in Rajkumari Devi’s household was characterised
by low productivity and limited diversification. Traditional cultivation methods were followed
without systematic soil management, crop planning, or improved cultivation practices.
Due to these limitations, the income generated from farming was insufficient to meet household
needs, forcing Rajkumari Devi to depend on daily wage labour for survival. Limited landholding
further meant that farming had to become more efficient and productive if it was to serve as a viable
livelihood.
3. Programme Intervention: Training and Sustainable Agriculture Practices
Rajkumari Devi’s farming journey began to change when she became associated with DEHAT,
which was facilitating farmer trainings and demonstrations on sustainable agricultural practices
across programme villages.
Through these training sessions and field demonstrations, she learned improved cultivation
practices, including seed treatment, soil fertility management, mulching techniques, irrigation
planning, and crop diversification strategies. These sessions also encouraged farmers to reduce
dependence on costly chemical inputs and instead use locally available organic resources.
Rajkumari Devi began implementing these practices on her own farmland. She adopted organic
nutrient inputs such as Jeevamrit and Cow Dung Manure (Gobar Khad) to improve soil fertility.
She also began using mulching techniques to conserve soil moisture and suppress weeds, which
is particularly beneficial for tuber crops. Additionally, she adopted line sowing methods, which
helped maintain proper plant spacing and made crop management easier.

4. Economic Transformation: Adoption of Turmeric Cultivation
A major turning point in Rajkumari Devi’s agricultural journey came when she experimented with
the Turmeric Cultivation on a small plot of land measuring less than one bigha.
Using improved seed, organic nutrient management, and better crop planning, she cultivated
turmeric while simultaneously adopting intercropping with Arbi (Colocasia) to optimise land use.
The use of organic inputs and improved cultivation techniques helped improve soil health and crop
performance.
This experiment resulted in a net income of approximately ₹60,000 from turmeric cultivation
alone, demonstrating the potential of improved farming practices even on small landholdings.
Encouraged by these results, Rajkumari Devi gradually expanded her farming activities. At present,
she cultivates Bananas on two bighas of land and turmeric on one bigha, generating a more
stable and reliable source of income for her household.
5. Programme-Level Impact: Turmeric Cultivation Across Farmers (2025)
Rajkumari Devi’s success reflects a broader agricultural shift
facilitated through DEHAT’s farmer support initiatives during
2025.
