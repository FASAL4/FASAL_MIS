1.  What were the challenges, (Organisational, programmatic, Financial) if any? How did you handle these? Is there any leadership change in the organization going forward? What is your plan for actively building second-line leadership?

**Leadership Vacuum and Transition:**

Challenge: The sudden passing of the founder during the second wave of COVID-19 created a leadership vacuum. This left the organization in a fragile state, especially with three out of four projects nearing completion. The transition to new leadership, which was young and female, was challenging, as the shift from a top-down leadership model to a collective leadership approach took time to implement.

How It Was Handled: The organization adopted a collective leadership model by establishing a Strategic Leadership Team (SLT) and a Core Team. These teams took on increasing responsibilities in strategic planning, decision-making, and program management, ensuring that the organization’s mission continued despite the leadership transition.

Leadership Change Going Forward: The leadership transition continues to be a work in progress. The focus is on building a resilient, feminist leadership culture that is decentralized and empowers teams to take ownership of their respective roles.

**Resistance to Policy Reforms:**

Challenge: The introduction of new policies, especially around financial management and human resources, faced resistance, particularly from older staff members. These policies were aligned with feminist values and challenged existing organizational norms, leading to pushback.

How It Was Handled: The organization engaged in ongoing communication and capacity-building efforts to help staff understand and align with these policies. Training sessions and external support were provided to ease the transition and integrate these reforms into the organizational culture.

**Human Resource Challenges:**

Challenge: The organization faced difficulties in attracting and retaining qualified, passionate programmatic and core staff. Its remote location, limited internet connectivity, and basic amenities made recruitment a challenge. Additionally, the high turnover of female staff due to safety concerns in intervention areas disrupted the continuity of activities.

How It Was Handled: The organization worked on building a support network for staff, particularly for female team members, offering gender-sensitive training and creating flexible work arrangements to address safety and work-life balance.

Efforts were also made to recruit individuals who align with the organization’s values and mission.

**Programmatic Challenges:**

**Resistance from Local Stakeholders:**

Challenge: Some local stakeholders, such as PDS contractors and village heads, resisted the program’s efforts to empower communities and led initiatives like Gram Sabhas. These stakeholders attempted to undermine the role of Community-Based Organizations (CBOs), which created significant barriers.

How It Was Handled: Strategic advocacy and dialogue were used to engage with local stakeholders and reduce resistance. The program highlighted the benefits of Gram Sabhas and the importance of CBOs in fostering accountability, leading to greater cooperation over time.

**Corruption and Exploitation:**

Challenge: The program faced corruption in accessing government schemes. Middlemen such as Lekhpals demanded bribes for essential documents needed for MNREGA job cards and other entitlements, depriving the vulnerable families of their rightful benefits.

How It Was Handled: The program strengthened the role of CBOs in advocating for transparency and accountability in government services. Community awareness campaigns and capacity-building initiatives empowered people to challenge corrupt practices and demand fair treatment.

**Financial Challenges:**

***Lack of Funds for Core Costs***

Challenge: The absence of funds allocated specifically for core operational costs created financial strain, making it difficult to maintain the core team and second-line leadership. This also impacted the continuity of program activities.

How It Was Handled: The organization reallocated available funds strategically, focusing on critical operational needs. Dialogue with donors emphasized the importance of flexible funding for core costs, and external partnerships were explored for additional funding sources.

***FCRA Compliance Issues***

Challenge: Delays in the FCRA signatory process led to the locking of the organization’s FCRA bank account, halting access to foreign funds and exacerbating financial challenges.

How It Was Handled: The organization worked closely with relevant authorities and the bank to expedite the signatory change process. Temporary internal measures were put in place to manage finances until access to funds was restored.

**Leadership Change and Plan for Building Second-Line Leadership:**

Leadership Change:

Plan: The young and feminist leadership is focusing on empowering the next generation of leaders within the organization. The transition is ongoing, and the organization is committed to strengthening collective leadership at every level.

Building Second-Line Leadership:

Strengthening Core Team and Strategic Leadership Team (SLT): The Core Team and SLT are being actively developed. These teams are taking on strategic decision-making roles and are expected to guide the organization in the future.

Capacity Building: The organization is working with external partners such as HIDRC and Janvikas to provide comprehensive leadership training for the Core Team and SLT, focusing on skills like management, advocacy, and financial sustainability.

Mentorship and Succession Planning: A succession plan is being developed to ensure that second-line leaders can step into senior roles as the organization matures. Senior leaders, in collaboration with external facilitators, are actively mentoring emerging leaders within the organization.

**Community-Level Leadership Development:**

The organization is also focused on empowering Community-Based Organizations (CBOs), which will serve as future leaders in their respective communities. The aim is to transition from DEHAT working for CBOs to working with CBOs as equal partners in community development, ensuring that leadership is not only centralized but also decentralized to community members.
