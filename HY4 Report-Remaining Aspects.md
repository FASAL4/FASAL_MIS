Despite the program's potential, the first   two   year s   of implementation revealed  several   significant   challenges   that   hindered   community   participation   and  adversely affected the success of FASAL's activities   to some level if not entirely .  A major issue within the community is alcoholism. The prevalence of excessive  alcohol use has been a significant barrier to effective participation in program

Many  families are dealing with domestic violence and other health issues directly linked  to alcohol abuse,   which further limits their ability to engage in FASAL initiatives.  These issues create a cycle of reduced participation and increased isolation,  making it difficult for affected individuals to benefit from the program.  Men's resistance to women's participation is another critical obstacle. In many  households, men hold significant power and influence over community decision -  making. Deep - rooted patriarchal beliefs dictate that men should be the primary  decision - makers, res ulting in resistance when women attempt to engage in  program activities. This resistance is often manifested through expressions of  masculinity, which prevent women from actively participating in meetings and  project   activities.   The   reluctance   to   include   w omen   in   these   initiatives  significantly reduces the impact of the program, as half of the community's  potential workforce remains underutili s ed.  Corruption   within   local   governance   and   administration   has   also   posed   a  substantial challenge. When local officials responsible for implementing legal  provisions and schemes are corrupt, it leads to the mismanagement of resources  and diminished trust in the   program. Community members become disillusioned  when they see resources intended to support them being siphoned off or  misallocated. This lack of trust further discourages participation and undermines  the effectiveness of FASAL activities.  Domestic violence remains a pervasive barrier that limits women's participation  in interventions. Women who are experiencing violence at home find it challenging  to participate in community activities, including the monthly meetings of the  Aajeevika Adhika r Sangathan (AAS). This isolation not only restricts their ability to  engage with the program but also prevents them from accessing essential  services and support that could help improve their situation. The fear of violence  and retribution keeps many wome n confined to their homes, unable to take  advantage of the opportunities presented by FASAL.  The pressure of household chores is another significant factor that limits women's  participation. Women are typically responsible for a wide range of domestic tasks,  including cooking, cleaning, and caring for children and elderly family members.  These res ponsibilities leave them with little time or energy to participate in  community activities and interventions. The burden of these chores not only  reduces their engagement but also limits the overall impact of the intervention, as  women are crucial to the s uccess of agricultural and community - based initiatives.  A lack of intersectoral coordination among government stakeholders has further  complicated the implementation of FASAL. Effective coordination is essential for  the successful execution of schemes related to agriculture and horticulture.  However, when diffe rent government agencies fail to coordinate effectively, it  leads to confusion and duplication of efforts. This lack of clarity and cohesion

among various agencies results in inefficiencies and missed opportunities,  hindering community participation and the overall success of the program.  Finally, the migration of young men and their partners, who are often part of AASs,  has also limited community participation. Many young men and their partners  leave the community in search of better opportunities, resulting in reduced  engagement and conti nuity in FASAL activities. This migration disrupts the stability  and impact of the program, as it loses active and potentially influential members  who could drive the initiatives forward.  The first   two   year s   of FASAL implementation ha ve   highlighted these challenges  that need to be addressed to improve community participation and ensure the  program's success. Tackling issues such as alcoholism, men's resistance to  women's   participation,   corruption   in   local   governance,   domestic   violence,  h ousehold responsibilities, lack of intersectoral coordination, and migration is  crucial for the effective execution of FASAL. By overcoming these barriers, DEHAT  is impr oving   community involvement, particularly among women, and achiev ing  the program's goal of improving the lives of small and marginal farmers. Through  concerted efforts and strategic interventions, the community   will   be   facil it ated   to  fully engage with and benefit from the initiatives designed to uplift them.
